Summary Statistics
Alpha (α)

What it is: The intercept — your portfolio's excess return after accounting for factor exposure. Annualized by multiplying daily alpha × 252.

Good: Positive and statistically significant (p<0.05) — you're generating genuine skill-based return.

Bad: Negative — you're destroying value vs. just holding the factors. Insignificant (p>0.05) means it could just be noise.

R-Squared (R²)

What it is: Fraction of portfolio variance explained by the factors. Ranges 0–1. Adj R² penalizes for adding factors that don't help.

Good: > 0.70 — your factors explain most of the portfolio's movement. Useful for understanding what drives your returns.

Bad: < 0.30 — your factors are missing something. Either the portfolio has unique/idiosyncratic risk, or you need different factors.

F-Statistic

What it is: Tests whether all your factors jointly explain portfolio returns better than a model with no factors at all (i.e., just the mean).

Good: Large F with p<0.05 — the overall model is statistically significant. At minimum, some of your factors matter.

Bad: F p-value > 0.05 — the model is no better than random. Your chosen factors don't explain the portfolio.

Durbin-Watson (DW)

What it is: Tests for serial correlation (autocorrelation) in the residuals. Ranges 0–4; ideal value is 2.0.

Good: 1.5 – 2.5 — residuals look independent. Your model isn't systematically missing a time-based pattern.

Bad: < 1.5 = positive autocorrelation (errors trend together). > 2.5 = negative autocorrelation. Both suggest a missing factor or regime changes.

Factor Table Columns
Coefficient (β)

What it is: The factor loading — how much your portfolio moves for each unit move in that factor.

Example: A Market β of 1.2 means your portfolio moves 1.2% for every 1% market move — you have 20% more market risk than the benchmark.

β > 1 = amplified exposure. β < 1 = dampened. β < 0 = inverse/hedged.

Standard Error (SE)

What it is: The uncertainty around the coefficient estimate. Smaller is better — it means the estimate is precise.

Rule of thumb: If the coefficient is less than 2× its standard error, it's probably not significant. (This is exactly what the t-stat checks.)

t-Statistic

What it is: Coefficient ÷ Standard Error. Tests whether this individual factor's coefficient is different from zero.

Good: |t| > 2 — the factor has a statistically meaningful relationship with your portfolio.

Bad: |t| < 2 — the factor might not be contributing. Consider dropping it (watch Adj R² to confirm).

p-Value

What it is: Probability that the coefficient is actually zero and we're seeing the result by chance. Lower = more confident.

*** p < 0.01 — very strong evidence
**  p < 0.05 — strong evidence (standard threshold)
*   p < 0.10 — weak evidence
n.s. p ≥ 0.10 — not significant

CFA convention: Use p<0.05 as the cutoff. Below it: reject the null (coefficient ≠ 0). Above it: fail to reject.

Residual Plot

What it shows: The part of your portfolio's returns that the factors can't explain — your idiosyncratic risk (or alpha, if persistent).

What you want: Random noise centered around zero, no visible pattern, roughly constant spread over time.

Red flags: Residuals trending up/down (missing factor), fanning out (heteroscedasticity), or clustered spikes (regime changes — the DW stat will also flag this).

How Leverage Works

At L× leverage, you borrow (L−1) dollars for every $1 of equity. A 2× portfolio borrows $1 per $1 owned.

Return: L × Rportfolio − (L−1) × Rborrow

Key insight: Returns and volatility scale linearly with leverage, but borrowing costs create drag that always reduces Sharpe below the unleveraged level.

Why Drawdowns Are the Danger

A 50% drawdown at 1× becomes a 100% wipeout at 2×. Drawdown scales worse than linearly because you need larger recoveries.

Recovery math: A 50% loss needs a 100% gain to break even. A 75% loss needs 300%.

Margin call risk: In practice, brokers require maintenance margin (~25–30%). A 2× leveraged portfolio faces a margin call after roughly a 37% drop.

Borrowing Cost Impact

Every unit of leverage above 1× incurs the borrowing rate. At 2× with 6% borrowing, you pay 6% annually in interest on the borrowed portion.

Sharpe degradation: Without costs, Sharpe is unchanged by leverage. With borrowing costs, Sharpe always falls as leverage increases — this is why the CML chart curves downward.

When Does Leverage Help?

High Sharpe + low borrowing cost — if your portfolio's Sharpe exceeds ~0.5 and you can borrow cheaply, moderate leverage (1.25–1.5×) can boost returns without catastrophic drawdowns.

Low Sharpe + high costs — leverage amplifies losses and the interest drag destroys remaining return. The table makes this obvious.

Value at Risk Explained
Parametric VaR

Assumes returns are normally distributed. Calculated as mean + z-score * standard deviation. Quick but may underestimate tail risk.

Historical VaR

Uses the actual percentile of historical returns. No distributional assumption. Limited by the sample period.

Monte Carlo VaR

Simulates 10,000 return scenarios using estimated mean and volatility. Flexible but depends on the assumed distribution.
